Our mountain bikes that we had were set-up tubeless since we were stayed in Arizona and New Mexico for many of our travels - thorny objects were always present.

This past year (2021) we replaced our mountain bikes with gravel bikes. We set-up both of our gravel bikes tubeless since we did not have any flats on our mountain bikes for two years.

### Gravel Bike One

On our way home from the bike shop with my wife's new Niner gravel bike we stopped for lunch. I happened to check the her new bike in the Kuat hitch rack after lunch (the Kuat rack holds the bike in wheel trays with both wheels on) and I found the front tubeless tire was totally flat.

Since I didn't have an air pump with me - we moved the bike into the back of the car. If I hadn't checked on the bike, there was a good chance that her new bike would have fallen off the Kuat hitchrack. Once I got the bike home, I added a few more ounces of sealant to the front tire and re-inflated the tire. For the next week both of her tubeless tires seemed to be okay.

During the next few weeks both tires on her bike continued to lose up to 10 PSI in a day. So I broke down and took the bike to our local bike shop to have the tires redone. For about three months the tires seemed to be holding air well. Then both tires started to lose 10 PSI between rides - every day or two. I added some more sealant and the tires held air better.

### Gravel Bike Two

My gravel bike tubeless tires have been even worse at losing air. I bought a used gravel bike that had just been converted to tubeless tires. Immediately the new tubeless setup was leaking air slowly. My first solution was to add more sealant - it worked for a few weeks. Next the sealant started seeping from around the tire beads - almost every day. I took the bike to the local bike shop to have the tubeless tires redone. New rim tape, valves, and sealant and the bike was good to go. The tires remained mostly worry-free until the rear tire needed to be replaced for wear. So, back to the bike shop for a new tire, rim tape, valve, sealant, and mounting.

I also invested in a Dynapug kit in case I got a tire hole that the sealant would not fix. There were three times my rear tire had a hole from debris that was too big for the sealant to fill and close. *Always fun getting tire sealant sprayed on your bike and backside!* The tire plugs did work very well and seemed to be a good solution for repairs on the road.

As the miles wore on so did the tires and I needed to replace both the front and back. I decided to try a different model of tire this time. The new tires seemed to hold air better and were trouble free for the first five (5) months. But then the rear tire stopped holding pressure and kept losing 10 PSI or more every day. For a time I just dealt with it and added air before every ride. Two months of adding air before each ride and I went back to my local shop to have the rear tire redone. My local shop spent two days getting the new tape job and sealant to cooperate with that rear tire. I got my tire/wheel back and finally thought my worries were over. One week later both of my tires started seeping sealant through the sidewall-bead area.

### Starting to Give Up

After a few weeks of wiping off sealant and reinflating the tires before every ride - I was ready to give up. I decided to just take some tubes and just put them in the tubeless tires. All I had to do was remove the existing tires, clean out the remaining sealant, remove the tubeless valves, and remount the tubeless tires with tubes.

It took me over 15 minutes to just break the beads on the first tire and remove it - yes it was a frickin' tight fit! Then remount and put in another tube took me another 15 minutes of agony. I had to brush on some liquid soap to get the bead to seat properly with tubes - ugh! Repeat and play this again with the second tire - I was not happy. I was doing this maintenace at home in a controlled environment and it wasn't easy. I couldn't imagine doing this task out on the road - I mean 30 minutes for one tire???

### I Gave Up

It was then I decided to go back to a normal clincher tires (not tubeless compatible) with tubes. I ordered new 700c x 42 Continental Speed Ride clinchers with new tubes. The new clincher tires and tubes arrived from UPS and installation was easy and uneventful- as it should be!

### Summary

So, I'm back on good old clincher tires with tubes - what I miss about tubeless tires:

* More comfortable ride with lower pressures - On the rougher stuff
* Less chance of flats - For small punctures that the sealant will fill
* Less chance of pinch flats - Definitely

What I don't miss about tubeless tires:

* Difficulty of installation - don't get me started, everything has to be just right
* Pain to maintain when problems happen - requires reinstalling over and over
* More expensive - tires cost more, plus special rim tape, valve cores, and sealant
* More complex - much more care in selecting rims with tires

### Back to Tubes

I went and rode some of our nearby gravel routes on the new tubed tires and I really didn't notice enough difference in ride quality and handling for all the headaches that I had with with tubeless tires. But I do know now if I flat, I can get a tube changed quickly and the tire will seat easily. Also if I had to boot a tire due to a larger cut - I would always prefer the non-tubeless tire, so much easier to mount.

I really like things that work (tubed tires) and I can maintain tubed tires and tubes without the all the tubeless Voodoo. The cost savings of tubed tires is also a big plus for a curmudgeon like me. I had been riding tubed tires for over 20 years and I am no longer convinced of the presumed benefits of tubeless on any bike other than a mountain bike.

Tubeless tires setup on our gravel bikes did not work for me. I have also converted my wife's bike to the same clincher tires with tubes and life is good!
